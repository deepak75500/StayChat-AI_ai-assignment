FOUNDER
The Taj Mahal Palace was conceived and built by Jamsetji Nusserwanji Tata
(1839–1904), the industrialist founder of the Tata Group, one of India's most
influential business empires. Tata had a vision to build a hotel worthy of
Bombay's global standing — one that would accommodate both Indian and European
guests on equal footing, at a time when most luxury establishments in British
India barred Indians from entry.

ORIGIN LEGEND
There are two accounts of why Tata built the hotel. The popular story holds
that Tata was refused entry at Watson's Hotel, Bombay (a European-only
establishment), and in response decided to build the finest hotel in the city.
Historian Sharada Dwivedi, however, argues that Tata built it "purely for the
love of the city" — his desire to reinvest in Bombay after the devastating Great
Epidemic (bubonic plague) of 1896. He wanted to restore the city's image as a
great cosmopolitan hub and attract foreign visitors.

CONSTRUCTION
- Foundation stone laid: November 1, 1898
- Architects: Originally commissioned to two Indian architects — Sitaram
  Khanderao Vaidya and D.N. Mirza. The project was completed by the English
  engineer and architect W.A. Chambers.
- Construction cost: 500,000 British pounds sterling (a staggering sum in 1903)
- Building material: Yellow basalt stone and reinforced concrete
- The iconic central dome was constructed using the same grade of imported steel
  used in the Eiffel Tower in Paris.
- Tata personally visited exhibitions in New York, London, Paris, Düsseldorf, and
  Berlin to source the most modern equipment available at the time.

OPENING
The Taj Mahal Palace opened on December 16, 1903, welcoming its first 17 guests.
The original room rate was six Indian rupees per night. The hotel opened before
the nearby Gateway of India was even built — the gateway was completed in 1924,
making the Taj older than its most famous neighbour by over 20 years.

The Indian Hotels Company Limited (IHCL) was incorporated in 1902 to own and
operate the property, and remains the parent company to this day.

FIRSTS IN INDIA
The Taj Mahal Palace was India's first property to offer:
- Electric lights and electric fans (powered by its own power plant)
- Four electric passenger lifts (elevators)
- A CO2 gas ice machine providing refrigeration and cooling
- A power laundry with electric irons
- A resident doctor and chemist's shop (pharmacy)
- Turkish baths
- An in-house post office
- Modern sanitation at Indian luxury standard

NOTABLE HISTORICAL MOMENTS
- 1903: India's first harbour landmark; the first sight for ships arriving at
  Bombay Port from the sea.
- Pre-Independence: The hotel hosted leaders of India's freedom movement —
  Mahatma Gandhi, Jawaharlal Nehru, Sarojini Naidu, Muhammad Ali Jinnah,
  Khan Abdul Ghaffar Khan, and Sardar Vallabhbhai Patel held strategic meetings
  here. The Tata family did not charge these leaders anything.
- 1947: Independent India's first speech to industry was delivered at the hotel.
  The hotel played a central role in post-independence celebrations.
- 1966: George Harrison checked in to take sitar lessons from legendary maestro
  Pandit Ravi Shankar. One suite (the Ravi Shankar Suite) commemorates this
  connection and houses the sitar on which Shankar composed his Concerto No. 1.
- Various decades: John Lennon and Yoko Ono stayed in the Rajput Suite for five
  days. Brad Pitt and Angelina Jolie have also stayed here. Barack and Michelle
  Obama stayed in the Tata Suite (reportedly ~5,000 sq ft) during a state visit.
- 2008: The hotel was one of the primary targets of the November 26 Mumbai terror
  attacks (26/11). The three-day siege caused severe damage to the Heritage Wing.
  Staff acted with extraordinary heroism, forming human shields and guiding guests
  to safety. 31 people died in the hotel; staff casualties were included.
- 2010: After a $37.3 million (INR 1.75 billion) restoration and reconstruction,
  the Heritage Wing reopened on August 12, 2010 — on India's Independence Day —
  grander than before. Consultants from the US, Italy, UK, India, and Singapore
  participated. Design firms included LTW Design Works, Lissoni Associati, and
  James Park Associates (JPA). Rockwell Group redesigned the Harbour Bar and
  Wasabi by Morimoto.

ARCHITECTURAL STYLE
The building is one of the most architecturally complex hotels in the world — a
deliberate fusion of multiple styles layered into a singular, unmistakable identity.
The style is formally described as "Indo-Saracenic Revival" or "Saracenic Revival
Architecture" blending:

  - Moorish (Islamic): Onion domes, horseshoe arches, geometric patterns
  - Florentine Renaissance: The iconic 240-foot-tall central red dome (Florentine
    in form, constructed with Eiffel Tower-grade steel)
  - Oriental / Mughal: Intricate latticework, jali screens, Rajput bay windows
  - Edwardian: Trellises, balustrades, stone detailing
  - Gujarati: Decorative trellises and ornamental stone carving
  - Rajput: Carved bay windows, traditional arched openings

KEY STRUCTURAL ELEMENTS
- Central Dome: 240 feet tall, Florentine red in colour, the defining landmark
  of the Mumbai skyline. It serves as an official daytime triangulation point for
  Indian Navy ships navigating the harbour.
- Two flanking wings topped with smaller subsidiary domes and cupolas.
- Facade in oriental style with elaborate carved stonework.
- Marble-floored lobbies with onyx columns.
- Grand staircases with wrought-iron balustrades.
- Ornate doorknobs and hand-finished hardware throughout.
- A long archival corridor that doubles as a heritage museum displaying
  memorabilia from the hotel's 120+ year history.
- Interior art collection: Over 4,000 works of art — most antique — including
  Italian marble, Belgian crystal chandeliers, Tanjore pillars, Udaipur-style
  relief panels, hand-knotted silk carpets, and four-poster beds.

HERITAGE WING (PALACE WING)
- Year Built: 1903
- Floors: 6
- Rooms: 285 rooms and suites
- Character: Old-world grandeur with period furniture, crystal chandeliers,
  silk drapes, hand-knotted carpets, and antique art.
- Entrance: The hotel's front entrance faces west (an architectural quirk —
  the rear faces the sea and has the spectacular sea views).
- The Heritage Wing was extensively damaged in the 2008 attacks and fully
  restored by 2010.

TOWER WING
- Year Built: 1970s (added as an expansion)
- Architect: American architect Melton Bekker
- Floors: 22
- Rooms: 258–268 rooms
- Character: Distinctly modern — muted tones, sleek contemporary furniture,
  floor-to-ceiling windows, and balconies framed by Indo-Saracenic arches.
- Features: Arched balconies with panoramic harbour views, Tanjore pillars
  and Udaipur-style relief panels in interior spaces.
- Views: Uninterrupted panoramic views of the Arabian Sea and Mumbai harbour.

A: Standard check-out is 12:00 PM (noon). Taj Club guests may check out until
   6:00 PM. Late check-out for standard rooms is subject to availability and
   surcharge.

Q: How many rooms does the hotel have?
A: The Heritage Wing has 285 rooms and suites (including 42 suites). The Tower
   Wing has approximately 258–268 rooms. Combined total is approximately 560
   rooms and 44+ suites.

Q: What restaurants are in the hotel?
A: The hotel has 9–10 dining outlets: Wasabi by Morimoto (Japanese), Golden
   Dragon (Chinese), Masala Kraft (Indian), Souk (rooftop, Eastern Mediterranean),
   Loya (North Indian), Shamiana (24-hour international), Sea Lounge (afternoon tea),
   La Patisserie (patisserie), Aquarius (poolside), Harbour Bar, and the
   Chef's Studio for private dining.

Q: Is there a pool?
A: Yes. The outdoor glass-edge swimming pool is open daily from 6:00 AM to
   10:00 PM.

Q: Is there a spa?
A: Yes. The J Wellness Circle (including Ayurveda Sanctuary) is open 8:00 AM
   to 11:00 PM daily. Prior appointment is required.

Q: Is Wi-Fi free?
A: Yes. Complimentary high-speed Wi-Fi is available throughout the hotel
   (up to 4 devices for standard Heritage Wing rooms; unlimited for higher
   categories). A premium bandwidth upgrade is available for an additional charge.

Q: Does the hotel have a gym?
A: Yes. The fitness centre with Technogym equipment is open 7:00 AM to 10:00 PM
   daily. Steam and shower facilities are included.

Q: What is the Tata Suite?
A: The Tata Suite is the hotel's grandest presidential suite on the 6th floor
   of the Heritage Wing — approximately 5,000 sq ft. It has 2 bedrooms (option
   to add a third), a meeting room for 15, grand living area, formal dining room,
   private office, lounge, private spa with sauna, private gym, and a butler's
   pantry. It was decorated to honour founder Jamsetji Tata.

Q: Who built the Taj Mahal Palace?
A: It was conceived and built by industrialist Jamsetji Nusserwanji Tata, founder
   of the Tata Group. It was completed by English architect and engineer W.A.
   Chambers (originally initiated by Indian architects Sitaram Khanderao Vaidya
   and D.N. Mirza). The hotel opened on December 16, 1903.

Q: What happened in 2008?
A: The hotel was targeted in the November 26, 2008 Mumbai terror attacks (26/11).
   The Heritage Wing was severely damaged in a three-day siege. Staff acted
   heroically to protect guests. After a USD $37.3 million restoration, the hotel
   fully reopened on August 12, 2010.

Q: Does the hotel offer airport transfers?
A: Yes. Paid airport transfers are available. Taj Club and Suite guests receive
   complimentary 1-way or 2-way Jaguar/Bentley transfers. Book through the
   concierge at least 24 hours in advance.

Q: Is there parking?
A: Yes. Overnight valet parking is available at an additional charge. Public
   parking is also available nearby.

Q: Can children stay for free?
A: Children aged 0–12 years can stay free using existing bedding. Free cribs
   and infant beds are available. An additional charge of INR 5,000 applies per
   extra guest beyond base occupancy in certain suite categories.

Q: What is the heritage walk?
A: The hotel offers a complimentary guided heritage walk through the building's
   history — led by Government-trained tourism officials. It covers the 120+ year
   history of the building, its architecture, art collection, and historical guests.
   Guests and even non-staying visitors may participate.

Q: What shopping is available at the hotel?
A: The hotel's in-premises shopping arcade includes high-end boutiques from Dior,
   Louis Vuitton, and Stefano Ricci, among others.

Q: Does the hotel accept credit cards?
A: Yes. Visa, Mastercard, American Express, and UnionPay are accepted. Cash in
   Indian Rupees (INR) is also accepted.

Q: What is the address of the Taj Mahal Palace?
A: Apollo Bunder Road, Colaba, Mumbai, Maharashtra 400001, India. It is located
   directly opposite the Gateway of India, overlooking the Arabian Sea.
